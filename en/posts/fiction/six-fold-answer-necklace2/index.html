Six-Fold Answer: The Necklace 2

Preface after Ten Years and Five Months

Ten years and five months ago, I read Paul Halter’s “The Seventh Solution.”

It was a revelation. Here was a mystery novel that not only presented a puzzle but also explored the nature of mystery novels themselves. It was meta-fiction at its finest, a story about stories, a mystery about mysteries.

I was inspired to write something similar, but I wanted to put my own spin on it. I wanted to create a story that was both a tribute to Halter’s work and something uniquely my own.

So I decided to write a sequel to Guy de Maupassant’s “The Necklace.” I would take the characters from that classic story and place them in a new mystery, one that would explore the themes of truth, deception, and the nature of reality.

The result is “Six-Fold Answer: The Necklace 2.”

I hope you enjoy it.

Foreword

This is a mystery novel.

It contains a murder, multiple suspects, and several different solutions to the puzzle. But it is also a story about stories, about how we construct narratives to make sense of the world around us.

The truth is not always what it appears to be. Sometimes the most obvious answer is the wrong one. Sometimes the real solution is hidden in plain sight.

So I ask you, dear reader: can you solve the mystery before the final revelation?

Good luck.

One

The dinner party was in full swing.

Mathilde Loisel, now Madame Forestier, sat at the head of the table, her elegant dress shimmering in the candlelight. She was still beautiful, though time had added a few lines to her face and a few pounds to her figure.

Her husband, Monsieur Forestier, sat beside her, his round face flushed with wine and good humor. He was a successful businessman now, having made his fortune in the textile trade.

Around the table sat their guests: Monsieur and Madame Loisel, Mathilde’s parents; Monsieur and Madame Ramponneau, old family friends; and Monsieur and Madame Leblanc, neighbors from down the street.

The conversation was lively and varied, ranging from politics to art to the latest gossip from Paris. The wine flowed freely, and the mood was festive.

Then, at exactly nine o’clock, the lights went out.

There was a moment of darkness, followed by the sound of breaking glass and a muffled cry. When the lights came back on a few seconds later, Monsieur Leblanc was slumped over the table, a knife protruding from his back.

He was dead.

Two

The police arrived within minutes.

Inspector Dubois, a tall, thin man with sharp features and sharper eyes, took charge of the investigation. He was known throughout Paris for his deductive skills and his ability to solve even the most complex cases.

“Ladies and gentlemen,” he said, addressing the assembled guests, “I need to ask you some questions. Please remain in the dining room while I conduct my investigation.”

He began with the basics: names, relationships, alibis. Each guest was questioned separately, their statements recorded and cross-referenced.

The facts were these:

  • The murder occurred at exactly nine o’clock
  • The lights went out for approximately ten seconds
  • Monsieur Leblanc was stabbed in the back with a kitchen knife
  • The knife was from the Forestier household
  • No one saw the actual murder
  • All guests claimed to be in the dining room when the lights went out

It was a classic locked-room mystery, with the killer apparently invisible and the weapon seemingly materializing out of thin air.

Inspector Dubois was intrigued. This was no ordinary murder.

Challenge to the Reader

Now, dear reader, I have a challenge for you.

I have presented you with the basic facts of the case. You know who was killed, where, when, and how. You know the circumstances and the suspects.

But I have not told you who the killer is, or why they committed the murder, or how they managed to do it without being seen.

That is for you to figure out.

I will present you with six different solutions to the mystery, each one plausible and well-reasoned. But only one of them is correct.

Can you identify the real solution before it is revealed?

Good luck.

Three

Solution One: The Jealous Husband

Monsieur Leblanc was having an affair with Madame Forestier. Monsieur Forestier discovered this and, in a fit of jealous rage, killed his rival.

The method: Monsieur Forestier turned off the lights, stabbed Monsieur Leblanc in the confusion, and then turned the lights back on. He was able to do this because he knew the layout of his own house and could navigate in the dark.

Evidence: Monsieur Forestier was the only one who knew where the light switch was located. He had motive (jealousy), opportunity (he was in the room), and means (it was his knife).

Solution Two: The Greedy Business Partner

Monsieur Leblanc was Monsieur Forestier’s business partner, and they had recently had a falling out over money. Monsieur Forestier killed him to gain full control of the business.

The method: Same as above, but with a different motive.

Evidence: Monsieur Forestier stood to gain financially from Monsieur Leblanc’s death. The business partnership was worth a significant amount of money.

Solution Three: The Revenge-Seeking Servant

One of the servants had a grudge against Monsieur Leblanc and used the opportunity to kill him.

The method: The servant turned off the lights from outside the room, entered through a side door, committed the murder, and then left the same way.

Evidence: The servants had access to the electrical system and knew the house layout. They could have entered and exited without being seen.

Four

Solution Four: The Mistaken Identity

The killer intended to kill someone else but killed Monsieur Leblanc by mistake.

The method: The killer turned off the lights, moved to where they thought their intended victim was sitting, and stabbed Monsieur Leblanc instead.

Evidence: Monsieur Leblanc was sitting in a different chair than usual, having switched seats with another guest earlier in the evening.

Solution Five: The Accidental Death

Monsieur Leblanc was not murdered at all. He accidentally fell onto the knife during the confusion when the lights went out.

The method: When the lights went out, there was a commotion as people tried to find their way around. Monsieur Leblanc stumbled and fell onto the knife, which was lying on the table.

Evidence: The knife was found in an unusual position, and there were signs of a struggle around Monsieur Leblanc’s chair.

Solution Six: The Perfect Crime

The murder was carefully planned and executed by someone who wanted to frame Monsieur Forestier.

The method: The killer turned off the lights, committed the murder, and then turned the lights back on, making it appear that Monsieur Forestier was the only one who could have done it.

Evidence: The timing was too perfect to be coincidental. The killer had to have known exactly when the lights would go out and how long they would stay off.

Five

Inspector Dubois listened to each solution with interest.

“Very interesting,” he said. “Each solution is plausible and well-reasoned. But there’s one problem: they all assume that the murder actually happened.”

The guests looked at him in confusion.

“What do you mean?” asked Madame Forestier.

“I mean,” said Inspector Dubois, “that Monsieur Leblanc is not dead. This was all a prank, orchestrated by him and his wife to test my deductive skills.”

As if on cue, Monsieur Leblanc sat up, removed the fake knife from his back, and smiled.

“Excellent work, Inspector,” he said. “You saw through our little deception.”

The guests were stunned. Madame Leblanc, who had been playing the role of the grieving widow, also smiled.

“We wanted to see if you could solve a mystery that didn’t actually exist,” she explained. “It’s a tribute to Paul Halter’s ‘The Seventh Solution,’ where the mystery is not what it appears to be.”

Inspector Dubois nodded. “I suspected as much from the beginning. The timing was too perfect, the circumstances too convenient. It was clearly a staged event.”

Monsieur Leblanc stood up and raised his glass. “To Inspector Dubois, the greatest detective in Paris!”

The guests joined in the toast, laughing at the elaborate prank that had been played on them.

Six

Later that evening, as the guests were leaving, Inspector Dubois pulled Monsieur Leblanc aside.

“That was quite a performance,” he said. “But I have one question: why did you choose me as the target of your prank?”

Monsieur Leblanc smiled. “Because you’re the best, Inspector. If anyone could see through our deception, it would be you.”

“And what was the point of all this?”

“To prove that sometimes the most obvious answer is the wrong one. That truth and fiction are not always what they appear to be. That reality is often stranger than we imagine.”

Inspector Dubois nodded thoughtfully. “You’re right. The real mystery was not who killed you, but whether you were killed at all.”

“Exactly. And that’s the beauty of meta-fiction - it makes us question not just the story, but the nature of storytelling itself.”

As Inspector Dubois walked home through the Paris streets, he thought about the evening’s events. He had solved a mystery that didn’t exist, proving that sometimes the greatest challenge is not finding the answer, but recognizing that there is no question to answer.

The six solutions he had considered were all clever and well-reasoned, but they were based on a false premise. The real solution was that there was no murder, no killer, no mystery.

Just a clever prank designed to make people think.

And in the end, that was perhaps the most satisfying solution of all.

Epilogue

The next morning, Inspector Dubois received a letter from Monsieur Leblanc.

“Dear Inspector,” it read, “Thank you for playing along with our little game. I hope you enjoyed it as much as we did.

“The truth is, we staged the entire thing because we wanted to create a story that would make people think. A story that would challenge their assumptions about mystery novels and the nature of truth.

“In the end, the real mystery was not about who committed the murder, but about what constitutes reality in fiction. We wanted to blur the line between truth and fiction, between reality and imagination.

“I hope we succeeded.

“Yours truly, Monsieur Leblanc”

Inspector Dubois smiled as he read the letter. Monsieur Leblanc was right - they had succeeded in creating a mystery that was more than just a mystery. They had created a story about stories, a mystery about mysteries.

And that, he thought, was perhaps the most interesting mystery of all.

The mystery of why we tell stories, and how those stories shape our understanding of the world around us.

The mystery of truth, and how we distinguish it from fiction.

The mystery of reality, and whether it is ever truly knowable.

These were the real mysteries, the ones that could never be solved by simple deduction or logical reasoning.

These were the mysteries that made life interesting.

And Inspector Dubois was grateful to Monsieur Leblanc for reminding him of that fact.
